Youth in politics:"2.8%: The Voice of Half the World”




If not you, then who? If not now, then when?
For generations, young people have been told that leadership is something to pursue later in life. Yet they are expected to inherit the consequences of today's political decisions. This raises an important question: if young people are trusted with the future, why are they so rarely trusted to help shape it?
-YUMNA OSAMA (Sudan)
Half of the world's population is under the age of 30, yet young people hold only 2.8% of parliamentary seats worldwide. At the same time, the average age of parliamentarians in most countries exceeds 50 years. This gap reveals a troubling reality: those who will live the longest with political decisions often have the smallest role in making them. In many societies, political and leadership positions remain concentrated in the hands of older generations. While experience is undoubtedly valuable, experience alone should not be the only qualification for leadership. When decision-making spaces are dominated by one age group, important perspectives can be overlooked Young people want to participate, but many face barriers that make political engagement difficult. They need supportive environments, meaningful civic education, and digital tools that allow real influence rather than symbolic participation. Young representatives bring fresh perspectives, digital skills, and future-oriented policies, yet eligibility requirements, stereotypes, and online harassment continue to limit their access to leadership positions. The situation is even more challenging for young women. The percentage of female parliamentarians aged 30 or younger has fallen to only 1.2% of all parliamentarians worldwide. As a result, many young voices remain unheard despite representing the experiences and aspirations of a large portion of society. When young people are excluded from political participation, democracy fails to represent everyone. More importantly, societies lose innovation, creativity, and perspectives that could help solve modern challenges. The deeper issue is not that young people lack the ability to lead. The real issue is that leadership is often associated with age more than competence. Islamic history offers a different perspective. The Prophet Muhammad (SAW) entrusted major responsibilities to young people when they demonstrated character, knowledge, and capability. Usama ibn Zayd was appointed to lead an army despite his young age. Mus'ab ibn Umayr was sent as an ambassador and teacher to Madinah, carrying one of the most important missions in early Islamic history. These examples show that leadership was not viewed as a reward for growing older, but as a responsibility given to those prepared to carry it. Young people do not need special treatment. They need opportunities. When youth are encouraged and supported, they become a powerful force for renewing and strengthening democratic societie

The future is not built by those who wait endlessly for permission. It is built by those willing to step forward and contribute. The question is no longer whether young people are capable of leadership. The real question is how much potential the world loses when their voices remain unheard.
